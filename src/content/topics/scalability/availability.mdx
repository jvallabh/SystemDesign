Availability is the probability that your system gives a useful answer when asked. It sounds like an operations metric, but it is really an architecture driver: write down a target like 99.99% and the math of how failures compose starts dictating where you need redundancy, how fast recovery must be, and how much you will spend. Most availability engineering is deciding which nines are worth buying.

## Counting the nines

Availability is quoted as a percentage of time (or of requests) served successfully. Each extra nine cuts allowed downtime by 10x, and the numbers get brutal fast:

| Availability | Nines | Downtime per year | Downtime per month |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| 99% | two | 3.65 days | 7.3 hours |
| 99.9% | three | 8.8 hours | 43.8 minutes |
| 99.95% | three and a half | 4.4 hours | 21.9 minutes |
| 99.99% | four | 52.6 minutes | 4.4 minutes |
| 99.999% | five | 5.3 minutes | 26.3 seconds |

The table explains a lot of industry behavior. At three nines, a human on call can notice an alert, log in, and fix things. At four nines you get about 4 minutes a month — a person paged at 3 a.m. cannot reliably beat that, so failover must be automatic. At five nines, even automated detection plus DNS propagation can blow the whole yearly budget in one incident. Five nines is telecom-switch territory; almost no web product genuinely needs it.

## SLI, SLO, SLA

Three terms that get conflated constantly:

- **SLI (indicator)** — the measurement itself, e.g. "fraction of HTTP responses with status under 500, measured at the load balancer, over a 28-day window." No target — just a measurement.
- **SLO (objective)** — your internal target for that SLI, e.g. "99.95% of requests succeed." This is the number engineering actually designs and alerts against.
- **SLA (agreement)** — the external contract with customers, with financial penalties attached. It is always looser than the SLO: the SLA is a promise, the SLO your safety margin. AWS EC2's SLA is 99.99% per region, with service credits if missed.

In practice: you alert on SLO burn, architect for the SLO, and negotiate the SLA.

## Why redundancy works: the composition math

This is the core mental model of the whole topic. Availabilities compose in two ways:

**Series** — if a request must pass through components A and B, both must be up: total availability is A × B. Every dependency you add multiplies your availability *down*.

**Parallel** — if a request can be served by either of two redundant replicas, the system fails only when *both* fail: total unavailability is the product of the individual failure probabilities.

Worked example. A request flows through a load balancer (99.99%), then three services in series (99.95% each), then a database (99.99%): 0.9999 × 0.9995 × 0.9995 × 0.9995 × 0.9999 ≈ 0.9983 — about **99.83%**, or nearly 15 hours of downtime a year, though every piece sounds excellent. This is why microservice sprawl quietly destroys availability: ten serial dependencies at 99.9% each gets you 99% overall.

Now the parallel case: two replicas at 99.9% each fail 0.1% of the time. Both down simultaneously: 0.001 × 0.001 = 0.000001, so the pair delivers 99.9999% — *if* their failures are independent. That "if" is the catch. Same rack, same AZ, same bad deploy, same expired certificate: correlated failure erases the parallel math — which is why clouds sell isolation boundaries (AZs, regions) and you spread replicas across them.

## Patterns that buy nines

- **Active-passive failover.** One node serves; a standby receives replicated state and gets promoted when health checks fail. Simple, but the standby is paid-for idle capacity, and failover itself takes seconds to minutes — classic for relational database primaries (e.g. RDS Multi-AZ).
- **Active-active.** All nodes serve traffic simultaneously; losing one just shrinks capacity. Better utilization and near-zero failover time, but every node must handle concurrent writes or be stateless.
- **Health checks.** Redundancy is useless if traffic keeps flowing to a dead node. Shallow checks (TCP accept) detect crashes; deep checks (execute a real query) detect zombies that accept connections but return garbage. Probe mechanics — intervals, thresholds, draining — live in the [Load Balancing](/SystemDesign/topics/scalability/load-balancing/) topic.
- **Multi-AZ and multi-region.** AZs protect against a datacenter failure; regions protect against regional outages (think AWS us-east-1). Multi-region active-active is the gold standard and also where [CAP Theorem](/SystemDesign/topics/distributed-systems/cap-theorem/)'s consistency trade-offs start to bite.
- **Graceful degradation.** A degraded answer beats no answer. Netflix serves unpersonalized fallback rows when its recommendation service is down; an e-commerce site can take orders while the reviews widget fails silently. Classify each dependency as critical or shed-able up front.
- **Circuit breakers and bulkheads.** A circuit breaker stops calling a failing dependency after repeated errors, returning a fallback immediately instead of stacking up timed-out threads. Bulkheads cap the resources (connections, threads) any one dependency can consume, so one slow downstream cannot exhaust the whole service. Both turn a dependency's outage into your degradation, not your outage; [Message Queues](/SystemDesign/topics/architecture-patterns/message-queues/) add a similar decoupling lever for async work.

## MTBF, MTTR, and error budgets

Availability = MTBF / (MTBF + MTTR): mean time between failures over total cycle time. The insight hiding in the formula is that **shrinking MTTR is usually cheaper than stretching MTBF**. A system that fails monthly but recovers in 30 seconds (99.999%) beats one that fails yearly but takes a day to fix (99.7%). This is why modern practice invests in fast automated recovery — health-checked failover, one-click rollbacks, chaos testing to rehearse failure — rather than chasing components that never break.

Error budgets, popularized by Google SRE, turn the SLO into a spendable resource. A 99.9% SLO grants roughly 43 minutes of unreliability a month. Budget intact: ship fast, take risks. Budget exhausted: freeze features and work on reliability. It replaces the dev-versus-ops argument with arithmetic, and makes explicit that 100% is the wrong target: users cannot tell 99.99% from 99.999%, but your feature velocity can.

## Trade-offs and pitfalls

- **Each nine costs roughly 10x.** Two nines is one decent server. Three nines is multi-AZ redundancy and on-call. Four nines is automated failover everywhere and no single point of failure, including your deploy pipeline. Five nines is multi-region active-active with fully automated response — and the redundancy machinery itself becomes a source of outages.
- **Failover that is never exercised does not work.** Untested standbys have stale configs, expired credentials, and unreplicated state. If you do not regularly fail over on purpose, assume you cannot.
- **Correlated failure is the silent killer.** The parallel math assumes independence; shared deploys, shared certificates, and shared control planes break it. Many famous outages took out "redundant" replicas with a single config push.
- **Averages hide the pain.** 99.9% measured yearly permits one 8-hour outage on Black Friday. Measure over short rolling windows, and consider request-weighted SLIs so peak-hour failures count more.
- **Complexity backfires.** Every failover controller, consensus layer, and retry policy is new code that can itself fail. Retries during a partial outage are a classic self-inflicted DDoS — cap them and add jitter.

## When to reach for it

Set the target first, from business impact, not pride: internal tools and batch pipelines are fine at 99.5%; a paid consumer product typically wants 99.9–99.95%; payments and infrastructure APIs justify 99.99%. Then let the composition math drive design — count your serial dependencies, multiply, and add parallelism (or remove dependencies from the critical path) until the arithmetic reaches the target. Buy the next nine only when its 10x cost is smaller than the revenue or trust the downtime would burn.
