A synchronous call couples two services in three ways at once: both must be up at the same moment, the caller must know where the callee is, and the caller can only go as fast as the callee responds. A message queue breaks all three couplings. The producer appends a message and moves on in a few milliseconds; the consumer pulls it whenever it is ready; the queue absorbs any difference between the two rates. That buffer is the entire value — and, unmanaged, the entire danger.

## Decoupling in time, space, and rate

Put a queue between an order service and an email service and three things change. **Time:** the email service can be down for ten minutes and no order is lost — messages wait. **Space:** the producer addresses a queue name, not a host; consumers can move, scale, or be rewritten without the producer noticing. **Rate:** a checkout burst of 1,000 orders per second does not force the email service to send 1,000 emails per second; it drains the queue at its own pace.

Two delivery topologies cover almost every use. A **point-to-point queue** delivers each message to exactly one of its consumers — work distribution, like a pool of workers resizing images. A **pub/sub topic** delivers each message to every subscriber — fan-out, like an `order-placed` event feeding email, analytics, and inventory independently. Kafka unifies the two with **consumer groups**: within a group, partitions are divided among members (queue semantics); separate groups each get the full stream (pub/sub semantics).

## Acks, visibility timeouts, and redelivery

The queue cannot know a message was processed unless the consumer says so. The core loop is: consumer receives a message, does the work, then sends an **acknowledgement**; only then does the broker delete it. If the consumer crashes mid-work, the ack never arrives and the broker redelivers the message to another consumer. Ack placement is the whole game — ack *before* processing and a crash loses the message; ack *after* and a crash duplicates it.

SQS makes the redelivery mechanism explicit with a **visibility timeout**. When a consumer receives a message, it is not deleted — it becomes invisible to other consumers for a window (default 30 seconds). Delete it within the window and it is gone for good; miss the window and it reappears for redelivery. Set the timeout shorter than your actual processing time and you get guaranteed duplicates: the message becomes visible again *while the first consumer is still working on it*.

Redelivery has a failure mode: the **poison message** — malformed payload, a bug triggered by one specific record — that crashes every consumer that touches it. Without intervention it redelivers forever, burning a consumer slot each cycle. The fix is a **dead-letter queue**: after N failed deliveries (SQS calls this `maxReceiveCount`, typically 3–5), the broker shunts the message to a side queue for humans to inspect. A DLQ with messages in it is an alarm, not a trash can — every entry is a request a user made that silently did not happen.

Kill a consumer mid-load and watch both depth and the age of the oldest message climb — age is the number your users feel. Then drop the visibility timeout below the processing time and count the duplicate deliveries.

## Delivery guarantees

| Guarantee | Mechanism | What goes wrong | Cost |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| At-most-once | Ack (or delete) before processing | Crash after ack loses the message | Cheapest; fine for metrics, telemetry |
| At-least-once | Ack after processing; redeliver on timeout | Crash after processing, before ack, duplicates it | Consumers must tolerate duplicates |
| Exactly-once | Transactions + idempotent producer (Kafka), dedup windows (SQS FIFO) | Guarantee ends at the broker's boundary | Throughput hit; false comfort if misread |

The exactly-once caveat deserves emphasis: Kafka's transactions make a consume-transform-produce loop exactly-once *within Kafka*. The moment your consumer calls an external API, charges a card, or writes to a non-participating database, you are back to at-least-once, because the side effect and the ack cannot commit atomically. The production pattern is **effectively-once**: accept at-least-once delivery and make the consumer idempotent, so duplicates are harmless. How to build idempotent handlers is its own topic ([Idempotency](/SystemDesign/topics/distributed-systems/idempotency/)).

## Ordering

Global ordering across a queue with concurrent consumers is fiction: two consumers processing in parallel finish in whatever order the scheduler decides, and a redelivered message arrives after messages that were sent later. Brokers therefore scope ordering. Kafka guarantees order **per partition** — all events for `user-42` hash to one partition and replay in write order, while different users interleave arbitrarily. SQS FIFO does the same per message group at a throughput cost (300 messages/s per queue without batching, versus effectively unlimited for standard queues). If you think you need global ordering, you usually need per-key ordering plus a version number.

## Backpressure: depth and age are the health metrics

A queue does not remove overload; it *relocates* it. If producers sustain 1,000 messages/s and consumers sustain 800/s, the queue grows by 200/s forever — an unbounded queue converts overload into delayed failure, at the moment of maximum traffic, with hours of stale work still buffered ahead of fresh work.

So the two metrics that matter are **depth** (messages waiting) and **age of the oldest message** (how stale the head of the queue is). They are linked by Little's law: time-in-queue equals depth divided by drain rate. Depth alone can mislead — 50,000 queued messages is fine at 10,000/s drain (5 seconds of lag) and a disaster at 50/s (17 minutes). Age is what the user experiences.

A worked spike: steady state is 200 messages/s in, consumers can do 400/s. A promotion fires traffic to 1,000/s for 60 seconds. The queue grows at 600/s, peaking at 36,000 messages. When the spike ends, consumers drain the surplus at 200/s (400 capacity minus 200 ongoing arrivals), taking 180 seconds to return to empty. Total user impact: the worst-placed message waits about 90 seconds (36,000 deep, drained at 400/s). No errors, no dropped orders, no autoscaling scramble — that is the queue doing its job. If the "spike" never ends, no buffer saves you: scale consumers, or shed load at the front door ([Rate Limiting](/SystemDesign/topics/scalability/rate-limiting/)).

## The broker landscape

**RabbitMQ** is the classic *smart broker*: exchanges route messages to queues by routing key, headers, or fan-out rules; the broker tracks per-message acks and deletes on ack. Rich routing, per-message TTLs, priorities — and messages are gone once consumed.

**Kafka is not a queue; it is a replicated log.** Messages are appended to partitions and retained for a configured window (days, or forever) regardless of consumption. Consumers track their own position as an **offset** and can rewind it — replay yesterday's events into a rebuilt cache, or point a brand-new service at week-old history. Deleting-on-ack versus retaining-with-offsets is *the* architectural distinction: a queue distributes work; a log records what happened. That is why Kafka underpins event sourcing and [CDC](/SystemDesign/topics/data-storage/cdc/) pipelines, and why a single partition can push hundreds of MB/s from sequential disk I/O.

**SQS** is the managed-simplicity option: no brokers to run, effectively unlimited throughput on standard queues, pay per request (about $0.40 per million after the free tier). You give up routing and replay; you gain never being paged for a broker disk filling up.

## Trade-offs and pitfalls

- **You traded latency and consistency for resilience.** The producer's "success" now means "enqueued", not "done". If the caller needs the answer in the response — auth checks, price quotes — a queue is the wrong tool; that boundary is covered under [Sync vs Async](/SystemDesign/topics/architecture-patterns/sync-vs-async/).
- **Debugging gets harder.** A request that fans out through three queues has no single stack trace. Budget for correlation IDs and tracing from day one.
- **Visibility timeout mismatch** is the most common self-inflicted duplicate source: processing takes 45s, timeout is 30s, every message is handled twice.
- **Unbounded queues hide sustained overload** until the backlog is hours deep. Alert on oldest-message age, not just depth.
- **A silent DLQ policy** (or none) means one poison message can wedge a FIFO group or waste a consumer forever.
- **Ordering assumptions creep in.** Any code that assumes cross-key ordering across a partitioned topic is a latent bug that appears only under rebalancing or redelivery.

## When to reach for it

Use a queue when the work is deferrable and the caller does not need the result in-line: sending email, generating thumbnails, syncing to a warehouse, fanning one event out to many services, or smoothing spiky writes into a fixed-capacity backend. Choose Kafka when you need replay, multiple independent readers of the same history, or event volumes in the hundreds of thousands per second; RabbitMQ when you need rich routing and per-message semantics at moderate scale; SQS when you want none of the operational burden and your needs are plain work distribution. Skip the queue entirely when the user is waiting on the answer — a queue in a synchronous path adds a hop, a failure mode, and an operational surface while removing the one thing the caller wanted: the response, now.
